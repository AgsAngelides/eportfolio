Part 1
We have been asked to read the Connor (2019) article and to answer the following: what is the point that Gearty Connor is trying to make?
The author is highlighting the fact that despite all the changes in the world, including those of technology, the law is still the same thing as it was before: it is based on rational thinking, on facts and that the law still matters to the world and it is still used by us to govern us.
We then had to read through Zeno-Zencovich’s book (2017) whilst focusing on the conclusion. When finished reading, we had to consider whether Zeno-Zencovich convinced us of his argument. In particular, we have to reflect on whether the approach of eliminating the conceptual distinction between legal systems can aid prevention and apprehension of cyber harm?
The author does convince me on the benefit of getting rid of the law labels (civil law/common law) and just taking things for what they are. I think the majority of the population does not distinguish between different types of law as is, and they are simply looking to correct the unjust done to them. Also, for example, if a cybercrime is committed by a gruntled parent over a divorce against another parent, would this fall under a family law or something else? The lines are now blurred more than ever and perhaps it no longer makes sense to put a case in one category or another.

Virtual mobbing is usually a social media platform based cybercrime during which a group of persons comment on another person because of, for example, opposing views (CPS, 2019), disability, religion, ethnics (Leymann, 1996), etc.

The concept of Mobbing was first used in Sweden by a doctor to refer to group of children directing destructive behaviour towards another child (Leymann, 1996).

Digital mobbing is not applicable to one group or an environment, and it has been observed in a workplace (Hladíková, 2020), professional education environment (Tura & Yardibi, 2018) and schools (Fischer et al, 2020), however, most of the mobbing research appears to be related to adolescents.

Mobbing, in this instance, refers to a long-term systematic humiliating, insulting, harassing of another person being targeted over a media platform (Uru & Gozukara, 2022). The mobbing, also sometimes referred to as cyber-bullying, can cause mental illness and other disorders and has been reported to result in many tragic events, including suicides (Lush, 2013).

Cyber mobbing purpose is similar to an offline, more traditional, bullying purpose: the aim is to harm another person, to humiliate them. Some aspects of the digital mobbing vary when compared to its physical counterpart: the online mobbing is not restricted to in-person hours, it requires a device and a connection to the internet to be successful, it allows for a larger group of people to participate in mobbing since it is no longer restricted to your community. However, since there is a trail of evidence left on all devices and social platforms, it also makes it easier for the victims to prove their suffering.

Some statistics in Canada also paint an interesting picture of how the traditional and cyber bullying overlap; it has been found that physical bullying declines as a person ages, whilst the cyber bullying/mobbing increases and peaks around ages 11 to 15 (prevent, 2022).

According to the UK CPS guidance, whether a crime is being committed by mobbing depends on a variety of factors, such as whether there is a credible threat involved, whether it is very offensive, indecent or if, for example, it is targeting an individual in a way which constitutes as harassment or stalking (CPS, 2019). Whilst the cyber mobbing is not a crime on its own, depending on the mobbing it may fall under a variety of laws within the UK.

Online mobs could be prosecuted in the UK under the Protection from Harassment Act 1997 since it is a criminal offence to harass another person regardless of where the harassment takes place (legislation.gov.uk, 1997).

The Malicious Communications Act of 1988 (legislation.gov.uk, 1988) and the Communications Act of 2003 (legislation.go.uk, 2003) are two other laws under which cyber mobbing is also a criminal offence.

If the virtual mobbing is happening at a work place, the Health and Safety Act of 1974 can be used to prosecuted the offenders (legislation.gov.uk, 1974).

Virtual mobbing could also be prosecuted under the civil laws in the UK. A good example of this is the case of Johnson vs Steele where a man was awarded £70,000 in damages over the online bullying conducted against him on Twitter by another person (Bailii, 2014).

Depending on whether there was a criminal offence committed or a civil one, the burden of proof will lie with a different person. If you were to sue a person(s) over virtual mobbing to some damages, you would have to be the one to prove the damage done in civil court (Holt et al, 2022). If the UK was to sue you over the criminal offences committed, it would it up the Crown prosecution services in the UK to prove you have committed the crimes (Holt et al, 2022).

Walden discusses how the rise in the cyber crime is having an impact on the balance between the right to investigate a crime and a right of privacy of a user, an organisation, service provider etc. And so, the author reviews particular instances where they feel this balance was achieved, such as having to pay for a personal data when conducting the investigation to ensure unnecessary requests are avoided or having to obtain a warrant for search on devices before carrying out the search.

I agree with the authors assessment that the balance has shifted towards the law enforcement vs the individual rights in the recent times. For example, the Amnesty International (2020) is pursuing a court case against the UK government over what appears to be a mass spying programme Tempora conducted by the GCHQ without, what appears to be, the governments consent or the knowledge of the country’s citizens.

Bryant & Kennedy (2014) in their paper go on to talk about how the investigation has moved away from trying to convict someone to trying to find the truth instead, i.e. the pursuit of information regardless of whether it points towards or away from the suspect. According to the paper, the law and policing were closely connected, because the miscarriages of justice performed by the police were used to enact new laws to ensure this does not happen again. For example, so that the police does not choose how they build a case or search for evidence or pick a suspect, but rather follow the guidelines as pert the law.

Although the authors talk about the state of policing many years ago, this relationship between the law and policing still applies today. The crimes are changing, and the tools used to commit these crimes. Many of the existing laws are no longer suitable to guide the law enforcement along the investigation and convictions, and so the police is often required to conduct the investigation based on what they think is right. Then, the mistakes made by the police may come up in a court case, which will set a precedent and will create new laws on how the same action should be carried out next time.

Much of this week's reading (Holt et al, 2022; Valjarević & Venter, 2015) focused on the forensic investigation techniques and the considerations (ethical and others) that the professional should have. It appears that whilst some of the processes exist on how to investigate digital evidence, a lot of it is derived from the physical evidence investigation techniques which is not always suitable. Furthermore, I found the concept of the 'Readiness' stage of the forensic investigation process interesting and useful. It's just a step to make sure that the organisations actually have the data stored in such way that it would enable the investigation without extra cost or impact on the business and it is interesting that I haven't seen this step in other literatures before. However, I would argue, that this is not an investigation part of process, but should rather part of internal organisation cyber policies.

Another important point made by the authors of one of the papers (Casey et al, 2009) was the need for a standardised system for assessing and evaluating cyber evidence promptly so not to delay the justice. Current lead times on evidence investigation due to the large volumes are too long for the justice system to work efficiently.

This unit made me question how well any country is prepared for handling any type of cyber crime. We live in a reactive world at the moment where things are changing so fast due to technological advances, that the there is not enough time to create new laws or update the existing ones to account for all the new types of crimes that exist and all the new ways it can be executed. So we are moving away from having laws written down which apply to the majority of the situations to assessing them on a case per case basis and setting the precedents for the future cases to come. Which means, that we are more and more reliant on the judges judgement and we can only hope that the judges who are now the main law enactors have the all the ethics, morals and technical knowledge to ensure the right decisions are made.

What are some of the problems law enforcement investigators (you can focus on the country of your choosing, or several countries) face when collecting digital evidence from a crime scene?

The paper by Badiya et al (2020) focuses on the issues related to the chain of custody and its importance to the evidence. The author highlights various important scenarios during which the chain of custody is essential and highlights the fact that it’s often viewed as a procedural burden rather than part of the evidence.

The problems discussed by Holt et al (2015) are all related to understanding the mind of the criminal. Holt analyses the problem of not having good theoretical models of providing the insight on cyber-criminal behaviour. Understanding how the criminal thinks, can help to determine the best way to collect the evidence and the best way to deter the criminal actions in the first place.

For example, if the investigators know the cyber criminals are more likely to come from certain environment, they could use that knowledge to narrow down the list of suspects.

Read Sunde and Dror (2019): and answer the following question: What are the issues raised in the article, and how these might affect further the validity and collection of evidence?

The paper by Sunder and Dror (2019) reflects on the issue of not having robust procedures for ensuring that the digital evidence is forensically sound and conducted to the highest scientific standards to ensure it does not contribute to wrongful convictions of innocent people. The authors discuss the issues that arise from the technology related errors as well as human and cognitive errors, placing the focus on the human related ones.

Specifically, the authors look into how three different types of the cognitive bias can impact the forensic specialist judgement: anchoring bias, availability bias and confirmation bias.

Other factors impacting the evidence relation discussed by the authors are environment, culture, experience, training, organisational, base rate expectations, irrelevant case information, reference material and case evidence.

All these factors which impact the cognitive bias can result in the evidence pointing to the wrong person and encourage bad evidence investigation practices which can in turn lead to wrongful convictions in court.

A smart motorway is a motorway where technology is used to manage the traffic by decreasing congestions and increasing the capacity. It aims to improve the infrastructure without damaging the surrounding environment and it is an economically viable option since it doesn’t require for the roads to be widened (Jallow et al, 2019). The first smart motorway was introduced to the UK in 2006 on the M42 and there are currently 14 smart motorways in operation today (Allan, 2022).

Whilst it would appear that the new type of motorway provides many benefits without incurring large costs, it has recently become one of the most debated topics within the UK.

The latest example of the ‘technology management’ gone wrong in terms of smart motorways was from the 28th of October in 2022, when the smart system malfunctioned and resulted in incorrect lane closures, wrong speeds and extremely bright variable speed signs blinding the drivers on a night drive (BBC, 2022), potentially endangering the drivers’ lives.

The smart motorway signage must be followed by all drivers at all times as it is legally enforceable (rac, 2022), this includes the speed limits and lane closures. However, what happens in the instance of the smart system malfunction as the one which happened in the UK recently? The Universal Declaration of Human rights (GRSP, 2017) and the UK Human Rights Act 1998 (Citisens Advice, 2022) both protect the person’s right to life, however, a faulty smart motorway system, the lack of hard shoulders and the UK driving laws enforcing the smart motorways driving suggestions endanger it.

There are several civil cases involving National Highways currently taking place where the families of the smart motorway victims are trying to get the justice for their loved ones and get the hard shoulders reinstated (irwinmitchell, 2022). As a result of these lawsuits, the rollout of new smart motorways has been paused. Hence, within the UK, the civil court can be used to protect the rights of the UK residents.
