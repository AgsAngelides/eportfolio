Network and Information Security Management


Introduction to the module

The 'Network and Information Security Management' module aims to provide the students with the necessary knowledge and hands on experience within the network technology subject. Concepts such as vulnerability assessments, penetration testing, forensic analysis and breach management will be discussed. The opportunity to evaluate ones personal development will also be provided.

Upon the completion of this module, students are expected to be able to:

  • Identify and analyse information security risks and vulnerabilities whilst also determining the methods, tools and techniques for managing/solving it at the same time.
  • Design and appraise computer programs for managing and auditing security issues.
  • Research and collate data from various security alert and warning sources to systematically analyse security breaches and issues.
  • Discuss issues faced by Cyber professionals, such as: legal, social, ethical, professional, etc.

Overview of the work completed

Unit overview

The Unit 1 focuses on the 4 security tenets (Availability, Confidentiality, Integrity, Non-repudiation) and its applicability to Information Security. It also provides theoretical knowledge on threats, risks and vulnerabilities, their classification, assessments and resolution types. The Information Security career path, potential roles (both technical and business needs related) are also reviewed.

The learning outcomes include being able to explain basic principles of Information Security Management, describing the mentioned 4 security tenets, describing what constitutes a threat and vulnerability and knowing some existing Information Security professions.

Learning outcome: to be able to research and synthesise data from various sources on security vulnerabilities and its mitigation.

The unit 2 aims to continue on from the unit 1; it further analysis the paper on medical mannequin with the following goals:

The preparation for the seminar 1 has asked us to revisit the vulnerabilities from the paper on the medical mannequin iStan and evaluate them using the DREAD risk analysis methodology we have learned in the lecturecast. The result of the research includes a very short Powerpoint presentation prepared by the group together (our Group 1 research and analysis results can be seen below.

The execution of this task has allowed us to meet our learning goals of being able to gather synthesise information from various sources as well as being able to identify and analyse security risks and vulnerabilities.

During the unit 2 we had to continue our discussions on the topic of the vulnerabilities applicable to the medical mannequin as reviewed by Glisson et al. (2015) and to prepare for the seminar 1. I had enjoyed the discussions and writing the peer responses because it had encouraged me to go and research more on the points mentioned by my peers so I was able to make my case. It has also further developed my skills in being able to collect information and collate it, which is one of the learning goals.
The preparation for the Seminar 1 was also interesting, since I was able to apply the knowledge gained in the lecturecat on the DREAD risk analysis method to an actual real life scenario. I think DREAD is a good risk analysis tool to introduce the students to as it is not complicated and it quickly demonstrates the risk analysis objectives without overcomplicating it. The trickiest part of this assignment was the team work. Since it's a non-assessed work, there is less motivation in the team to do the research and even less motivation to do it on time for the seminar. However, we have agreed that the team members who cannot participate in the seminar will do the majority of the data collating work, whilst those able to attend the seminar would present.
We have also had some discussions within the team where we could not quite agree on the DREAD score for the exploitability category for the 802.11 wireless transmissions related vulnerability. Some of the team felt that the exploitability should be ranked at 3, since Microsoft documentation defines 3 (Meier, 2003) as something that an unexperienced hacker could do. Whilst the other team members felt that the Exploitability should be ranked at 2 - since they felt the exploitability should consider factors such as the perpetrator must be at a close proximity to the router making it harder to exploit the said vulnerability. Eventually, we ended up with a 2/3 - a 'meet in the middle' score and decided to explain our discussion on the seminar instead of trying to reach one score.

Unit overview

During the week 3 (Unit 3) of the module, we had:

  • Learned a brief history of networks, ISO/OSI 7 layer model and how it translates to the TCIP/IP layers.
  • Learned and applied basic command line statements such as ping, nslookup and tracert (or ping, traceroute and dig for Linux) on Windows
  • Learned about IPv4 and IPv6 and its differences.

We have been asked to perform a basic scan of a website assigned to our team (Group 1). We had to use the tools available online or the command line to obtain various information such as: the number of hops, hops with high latency, website webservers, etc.

The learning goal here is to be able to identify security risks and vulnerabilities (e.g. network topology mapping) using the tools available (e.g. traceroute).

The Unit 3 consisted of a few main tasks. I had a clear idea of what I wanted to write about in my final discussion forum post, since I have received valuable peer contributions that pointed out some things I have initially misunderstood in the paper on Medical Mannequin (Glisson, 2015). Other peer initial posts and responses on their posts have also helped me to understand other vulnerability mitigation options that there are. On the other hand, I found the individual scans we had to complete on the website assigned to us more challenging. Unit overview

In the unit 5 we are looking at the various network components (switches, routers, firewalls, etc), when and how to use it as well as any related security implications. We are also going to be learning about the role of these components in the cloud environments and discussing network diagnostic tools such as packet sniffers and scanners.

For the seminar 3 preparation, we have been asked to read a blog post “Geer, D. (2015) 8 Penetration Testing Tools That Will Do the Job. (Network World). All pen security tools in the post were then rated by myself from 1 to 5 (5 being the highest/most popular) in the following categories: Ease of install, Ease of use, Flexibility, Licensing and Reputation.

I have used 1 for Reputation when the tool was new and not known, and 5 for well-established tools.

When it came to licensing, I rated open-source free tools at 5, and paid ones based on their price, 1 being the most expensive one.

When it came to flexibility, I rated it based on the OS each tool supports and the amount of configuration it allows the user to do.

Ease of use was rated based on how much automation each tool provided and if it required other additional knowledge to run it, e.g. know Linux command line, etc.

I have also removed the Privacy rating from my scores, as I felt it was unnecessary and confusing.

The highest rated tools in my assessment were Metasploit and OWASP ZAP. It seems that it came down to price and the ease of use; i.e. the paid tools lost a lot of points in my assessment. The Linux only based tools were the lowest rated ones in my case, because they were limited to being used on one OS only, despite being free and having a good reputation. Also, Linux only tools require Linux knowledge, which is also another downside, in my opinion.

Unit overview

The unit 6 focuses on the selection of the scanning toolset to use on a webapplication. The reading materials for this unit are also meant to assist us with making well though through decisions on this subject. In this unit we are also to submit our first graded assignment as a team as well as the peer reviews. The assignment requires us to create a design document on the website assigned to our team (Group 1) which reviews governing bodies applicable to the website, vulnerabilities related to these governing bodies and proposes the mitigation options ranked using the business priorities.

We scheduled a mid week meeting to go over the scores we gave to each pen test tool discussed in the Geer, D. (2018) blog post based on the following criteria: ease of install, ease of use, flexibility, licensing, privacy and reputation.

Most of us struggled to grasp the meaning of the 'Privacy' criteria. We were not sure what was meant by it, but eventually, we ended up scoring each tool based on whether it is an open source tool (less privacy) or paid tool (more privacy in most cases). Also based on whether the scan data is shared or storedd locally.

During the meeting we agreed on team scores for each pen testing tool and scoring category combination, worked out the totals and averages.

In the end, our team member Hamad hhhas kindly volunteered to surmise all details in the .ppt document ready for the upcoming seminar.
