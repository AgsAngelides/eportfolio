Information Risk Managament


Introduction to the module

This module aims to introduce the students to the Principles of the Information Risk Management, its assessment and mitigation techniques. In summary, the purpose of this module is to provide:

  • A basic understanding of the Information Risk Management principles.
  • An understanding of the relationships between the Information Risk management and the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC).
  • An understanding of the residual risks in the SDLC.
  • An introduction into the current and future chalanges, limitations and opportunities.

My response to Anoushka's post is below.

Hi Anoushka,
I absolutely agree, and as I mentioned in my original post, there are many disadvantages of using assumptions when producing a risk assessment.

Your proposed threat modelling and risk assessment methods are widely used and accepted among the professionals, however, these still rely on the assumptions in the absence of access to the users and data. Thus, the results produced by these tools could still fall short in producing anything of use.

However, as the paper Frage & Askeland (2020) points out, assumptions are unavoidable in the quantitative data analysis, when qualitative data analysis is not an option: assumptions are used in every risk assessment. However, as long as the risk analyst is aware of the assumptions and the potential risks associated to those and address it as part of the risk assessment report, the decision makers will have a better picture and will still be able to make better justified decisions.

Introduction

Acme Manufacturing has decided to purchase an ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) system and is considering three options:

  • A commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) solution.
  • An open-source (OS) solution.
  • An in-house developed solution.

Company Profile and Risk Appetite

Acme Manufacturing is a small-to-medium sized enterprise (SME), employing 150 staff. They currently use spreadsheets to track and manage resource planning and scheduling. Based upon research into similar organisations, this report makes the following assumptions:

  • The company has a small IT team where every person performs multiple tasks (Malhotra & Temponi, 2010).
  • The company has a flat hierarchy, enabling faster decisions (Poba-nzaou & Raymondl, 2010).
  • Acme Manufacturing is profitable; annual turnover up to £25m (Clark, 2022).
  • The company prioritises steady over aggressive growth (Yap & Ng, 2015).
  • The company is privately owned, based on the SME UK statistics (Office for National Statistics, 2018).
  • The company uses a functional organizational structure; the tasks are divided into functional units where each unit handles one aspect of the product/service (Kovaci et al, 2021).
Based upon this profile, according to the UK SMEs Survey by Studio Graphene (Jones, 2021) Acme Manufacturing is likely risk averse and possesses a low-risk appetite.

Business risks

  • Lack of employee buy-in can negatively impact ERP implementation time, effort, and cost (Malhotra & Temponi, 2010).
  • Poorly implemented ERP systems may not provide return on investment and impact performance (Quiescenti, 2006; Summer, 2000).
  • SMEs are less likely to survive/overcome a failed ERP implementation (Muscatello et al., 2003).

Recommendation and Conclusions

Based on the identified requirements and risks, we recommend the open-source solution.

The benefits of open-source ERP applications exceed the potential disadvantages (see Table 1):

  • Tools are more customisable to support business needs (Johansson & Sudzina, 2008)
  • Implementation can be scheduled around the business roadmap (see Appendix A)
  • The cost is lower (Riehle, 2007) resulting in a lower failure cost
  • The IT team, after training, is likely to provide prompter support (Chang, 2004)
  • Disaster recovery objectives may be easier to satisfy (see appendix B), especially if an open-source cloud-based solution is used (CompuData, 2019)

Risk Management Challenges

Question
How can we accurately represent and account for uncertainties in a way that properly justifies confidence in the risk results?

Response
Risk management is often a highly subjective practice, especially in the industries within which there isn’t much data to rely upon. This means, that any risk management predictions made are likely to have a high degree of uncertainty, which must be made clear to the decision makers when presenting the risk analysis results.
The projects (especially the ones that take longer to complete) are also constantly changing, so there is no way of accurately measuring the uncertainty of a risk due to the ever changing project landscape (Perminova, 2008). Thus, various different authors suggest that the risk management should be performed as part of every SDLC stage, rather than being completed as an independent activity (Jaafari, 2001; Perminova, 2008); this would not only reduce the uncertainty resulting from various assumptions but would also allow for a better risk probability/impact analysis, thus allowing a more accurate uncertainty representation.

References
Jaafari, A. (2001). Management of risks, uncertainties and opportunities on projects: time for a fundamental shift. International Journal of Project Management, 19(2), pp.89–101.
Perminova, O., Gustafsson, M. and Wikström, K. (2008) Defining uncertainty in projects – a new perspective. International Journal of Project Management, 26(1), pp.73–79.

Question 2
Based on Aven (2016) and previous papers (supplied as part of the reading list for unit 5), select the one trend that you believe will be the main factor driving the development of the information management (IRM) field in the next five years.

Response
For this question, I have selected to further research the Real Options Theory (ROT) as one of the current trends in the Information Risk Management (IRM).
The ROT framework allows the management teams to make investment decisions when the investment is accompanied by an uncertainty. The ‘real option’ in this theory’s title refers to a tangible asset (e.g. in the Information Technology world, this could be a server, an ERP system, etc.) and the cost associated to acquiring it, thus allowing the management team to estimate the opportunity cost and to decide whether to go ahead with the project or abandon it (Chi et al, 2019). In other words, it refers to the opportunities that the business may or may not take.
In this theory, each asset is linked to certain conditions, a period of time and it has an expiration date. If all the conditions are met, the investor can either go ahead with the opportunity and invest the amount associated to the asset which meets those conditions or they can reject the opportunity (Chi et al, 2019).
Whilst this theory emerged in the finance world, it has been widely applied in other industries: it has been used to manage software architecture scalability related uncertainty (Bahsoon & Emmerich, 2004) and software product/project development uncertainty (Sullivan et al, 1999).
