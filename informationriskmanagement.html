Information Risk Managament


Introduction to the module

This module aims to introduce the students to the Principles of the Information Risk Management, its assessment and mitigation techniques. In summary, the purpose of this module is to provide:

  • A basic understanding of the Information Risk Management principles.
  • An understanding of the relationships between the Information Risk management and the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC).
  • An understanding of the residual risks in the SDLC.
  • An introduction into the current and future chalanges, limitations and opportunities.

Unit overview

The aim of the unit 1 is to review the common definitions of the Information Risk Management and other relared terminologies, discuss related Security Management Concepts and the Risk Management Process (the risk assessment, qualifying and mitigation). We have also reviewed various approaches of quantifying and qualifying the risk.

The first week of the Information Risk Management module was about us getting introduced to our tutor, touching upon the upcoming assignments, starting to learn some basics on the Risk Management in terms of information security and to get to know our new teams.

The team allocation did not happen until much later in the week, which meant that we lost quite a bit of time in working out the logistics on how we are to communicated, everyone's availability, etc. The teams were also not assigned this time and we had to rather provide our own preference. Which can be good too, but also, it was the reason why the team assignment took so much time. I think it would have been better if we were assigned teams at the beginning of the week rather than having to wait for seminar to find out how the team assignment process will happen and then having to wait a few more days after that.

The reading in this unit included the first three chapers of the Sutton (2021) book on the Information Risk Management, the Hubbard book (2009) on the failure of risk management and the risk management guide produced by NIST (2002). I found the book by Hubbard to be quite controversial: I do not think I agree with his statements that non quantitative risk assessment methods used by the management teams of various companies is a terrible idea. Insurance, finance companies, etc. have a lot of pre-existing data which they are often very protective of which can be used in calculating the risk likelihood, impact, cost, etc. However, for many smaller or medium sized business this data does not exist. The risk management still needs to performed based on the best assumptions and so the low, medium, high qualifications to provide some value, in my opinion.

The document published by NIST was also very informative. The risk management guidance provided in the document was very clear and informative and could be easily followed by less experienced professionals who need to implement risk management systems. I think this guidance will also be useful when completing our team assignments.

My response to Anoushka's post is below.

Hi Anoushka,
I absolutely agree, and as I mentioned in my original post, there are many disadvantages of using assumptions when producing a risk assessment.

Your proposed threat modelling and risk assessment methods are widely used and accepted among the professionals, however, these still rely on the assumptions in the absence of access to the users and data. Thus, the results produced by these tools could still fall short in producing anything of use.

However, as the paper Frage & Askeland (2020) points out, assumptions are unavoidable in the quantitative data analysis, when qualitative data analysis is not an option: assumptions are used in every risk assessment. However, as long as the risk analyst is aware of the assumptions and the potential risks associated to those and address it as part of the risk assessment report, the decision makers will have a better picture and will still be able to make better justified decisions.

Introduction

Acme Manufacturing has decided to purchase an ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) system and is considering three options:

  • A commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) solution.
  • An open-source (OS) solution.
  • An in-house developed solution.

Company Profile and Risk Appetite

Acme Manufacturing is a small-to-medium sized enterprise (SME), employing 150 staff. They currently use spreadsheets to track and manage resource planning and scheduling. Based upon research into similar organisations, this report makes the following assumptions:

  • The company has a small IT team where every person performs multiple tasks (Malhotra & Temponi, 2010).
  • The company has a flat hierarchy, enabling faster decisions (Poba-nzaou & Raymondl, 2010).
  • Acme Manufacturing is profitable; annual turnover up to £25m (Clark, 2022).
  • The company prioritises steady over aggressive growth (Yap & Ng, 2015).
  • The company is privately owned, based on the SME UK statistics (Office for National Statistics, 2018).
  • The company uses a functional organizational structure; the tasks are divided into functional units where each unit handles one aspect of the product/service (Kovaci et al, 2021).
Based upon this profile, according to the UK SMEs Survey by Studio Graphene (Jones, 2021) Acme Manufacturing is likely risk averse and possesses a low-risk appetite.

Business risks

  • Lack of employee buy-in can negatively impact ERP implementation time, effort, and cost (Malhotra & Temponi, 2010).
  • Poorly implemented ERP systems may not provide return on investment and impact performance (Quiescenti, 2006; Summer, 2000).
  • SMEs are less likely to survive/overcome a failed ERP implementation (Muscatello et al., 2003).

ERP Options Risk Assessment

ERP options Applicable risks
COTS Solution
  • R1 - Due to inflexibility, the COTS solution may not support all business requirements (Malhotra & Temponi, 2010).
  • R2 - New versions can incur additional implementation costs (Babey, 2006).
  • R3 - COTS tend to require a high level of costly customisation (Babey, 2006; Quiescenti, 2006).
  • R4 - Total costs of a COTS solution often reduce annual profits (Holland & Skarke, 2001).
  • R5 - Increases reliance on a single vendor and inherits any vendor security issues (Tasnawijitwong & Samanchuen, 2018).
  • R6 - 66% to 70% of ERP implementations fail (Poba‐Nzaou et al, 2008) leading to significant financial loss if COTS solution is used.
  • R7 - Many known COTS providers do not support the DR requirements (QuickBooks, 2011; OracleNetsuite, 2022).
Open-source solution
  • R1 - No vendor support available (Olson et al, 2018).
  • R2 - Lack of documentation, compliance with industrial standards, liability responsibility (Olson et al, 2018).
  • R3 - High level of customisation, if implemented, can introduce security vulnerabilities (Ashraf, 2019).
  • R4 - Open-source libraries often introduce security vulnerabilities (Veracode, 2021).
  • R5 - Open-source ERP could have higher IT costs (Economides & Katsamakas, 2006, as cited in Johansson & Sudzina, 2008 ).
In-house solution
  • R1 - Inexperienced personnel can take a long time to implement an in-house ERP solution (Malhotra & Temponi, 2010).
  • R2 - Potential security risks as the developer is inexperienced (Amroussi, 2022).
  • R3 - The system may become unmanageable if the developer leaves the organisation (Shahzad et. al., 2017).
Table 1: The ERP options and their applicable risks.


Recommendation and Conclusions

Based on the identified requirements and risks, we recommend the open-source solution.

The benefits of open-source ERP applications exceed the potential disadvantages (see Table 1):

  • Tools are more customisable to support business needs (Johansson & Sudzina, 2008)
  • Implementation can be scheduled around the business roadmap (see Appendix A)
  • The cost is lower (Riehle, 2007) resulting in a lower failure cost
  • The IT team, after training, is likely to provide prompter support (Chang, 2004)
  • Disaster recovery objectives may be easier to satisfy (see appendix B), especially if an open-source cloud-based solution is used (CompuData, 2019)

This week we have continued on reading more research papers, whilst focusing on the risks associated to the Software Development Lifecycle. The two papers by Hijazi et al. (2014) and Roy et al. (2015) have provided a very detailed overview of a variety of risks that fall under each software development stage. The papers have also reviewed the overal risk categories that should be used when conducting a risk assessment, to ensure no certain type of risk is underlooked.
The papers do provide a good guidance and could be used when assessing risks associated to any new software development. Ideas discussed by both authors are similar and discuss the need of addressing the risks applicable to each software development stage, rather than an overall risk, as a means of ensurring that the all types of risks are analysed.
I don't see any downsides to the proposed risk analysis methodology and I can see it working well in the real-world situations, given my own Project Management experiences. However, I do think that these risk analysis methods are less known, even though they have a lot to offer, and that potentially the Information technology community should look at developing the risk analysis framework most suitable to this particular industry.
