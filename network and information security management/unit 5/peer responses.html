Internet providers often use IATA airport codes to identify the geographic location of routers on their network (Lamberti, 2020). In the tracepath, you can see that my local Internet Service Provider hands off my packets at hop 6 to Cogent Communications. Cogent clearly uses IATA codes, so you can read the path from hop 6 in Toronto (YYZ) to hop 7 in Montreal (YMQ) to hop 8 in Liverpool (LPL) to hop 9 in Amsterdam (AMS). At that point, Cogent hands the packets off to A2 Hosting for the last mile to our target server. If the packets continued back from Amsterdam to the USA, we would see additional hops after that.

It is also important to note that my longest delay occurs between 7 and 8, which is a trans-Atlantic hop from Montreal to Liverpool. Routing on the Internet is controlled by the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP). BGP uses many different factors to select a routing path, but generally speaking it tries to select the shortest, fastest, and/or most efficient route (IPXO, 2022). If the site really is in the USA, it would be illogical for my packets to go from Canada to the UK to the Netherlands, and then back across the ocean to the USA.

Hi Jurbe.

Zorz (2019) describes how cognitive biases can undermine cybersecurity programs. Particularly relevant for this topic is anchoring and confirmation bias. Anchoring bias describes our tendency to accept the first piece of information that we come across as the absolute truth, and confirmation bias describes our tendency to only look for evidence that fits our theories. In this situation, I cannot help but feel that a number of people have performed a web-based IP address geolocation on the site and have 'anchored' onto the result that the site should be in the USA. Confirmation bias has then prevented them from critically examining this anchor, and instead caused them to look only for data that supports this theory.

To address your specific point, Yacobi-Keller, et. al. (2009) describe how IP addresses associated with a single autonomous system (AS) need not be in a single country or even a single continent.

Hi Jurbe,

thank you for your response.

When I did my basic scans and analysed my traceroute results, I was of the same opinion to start with - I could swear that the web server was hosted in the US. However, as Kevin has mentioned up above, the IP geolocation lookup tools should probably be used more carefully and the location provided should be verified against other factors (delays between hops, router titles).

Whilst researching on this subject to prepare for team discussion, I found quite a lot of information which explains how these location lookup services work and why they often lack accuracy. Dan et al. in their paper (2021) on the IP geolocation lookup through reverse DNS does a very good job at explaining it. For example, most of these location lookup services use a topology or a network delay method. The latter issues pings to your provided IP address from different geographical locations, measures the delay and then triangulates into an approximate location. However, routes taken to the target IP address do not always correspond to geographical routes, there are also scalability issues (many servers are required to conduct multiple pings to the same IP address for accurate results). Due to these reasons, you often get an incorrect location when using a geolocation lookup tools available.

There is also IP availability issue to consider. Many companies lease IP addresses from a pool of IP addresses available in another company. These leased addresses will be registered to the service provider company rather than the company leasing it, and so if the IP location lookup tool is using the registered address, then you would get the address of the service provider rather than the actual location of your device (a2hosting, 2022).
