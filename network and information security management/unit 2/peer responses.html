Hi Beran,

I agree with the theoretical benefits of the implementation of the zero-trust architecture in the healthcare. As we know, the healthcare data is very valuable in the black market fetching up to $250 per record (Taylor, 2022) because of its use in creating false identities (Campbell, 2016). Hence, authenticating devices and users, monitoring all devices and network, encrypting the network, segmenting the network to wall off vulnerable devices makes sense.

However, there are some concerns that instantly come to my mind. A mandatory authentication of every person/device trying to connect to the internal healthcare organisation network could create unnecessary red tape: a medical professional putting in some extra hours of work from home trying to connect to the hospital's network, visiting professionals trying to access patient records, an undetected legacy device trying to connect (the equipment which cannot be securely connected to the network because of its age/outdated OS, etc. (Slabodkin, 2021)), etc. There is also always a concern over an incorrectly implemented architecture and the impact it could have on patients. For example, an incorrectly configured firewall used to segment the network to separate legacy medical equipment could cut off these devices temporarily making them unavailable and impacting the patients (Tyler, 2021).

So whilst it does carry a lot of theoretical benefits, in my opinion, there are many places where things could go wrong due to the complexity of healthcare organisations, the number of legacy devices which cannot be integrated, and so risk/reward ratio should be assessed for each new control, process and/or policy.

My response to Kevin's post.

Hi Kevin,

thank you for sharing your thoughts. I think you made some good points, since the attack was carried out directly on the devices 802.11 wireless access point (Glisson, et. al., 2015), a firewall would not have helped here. Thank you for pointing this out.

However, a firewall placed between the mannequin and the machine that controls it (and which, I assume, connects to the main network), could still be helpful. For example, the firewall could be configured to only allow a one way traffic from the machine to the mannequin to permit for the mannequin to be configured (essentially segmenting the mannequin away from the main network) (Liebetrau, 2013). The communication from the mannequin to the machine could then be disallowed to reduce a possibility of a hacker gaining access to the network via a rogue access point.

Another option is to introduce physical security controls since the wireless access point scanners generally only have a range of up to 45m indoors (Tanaza, 2017). For example, only employees and students with employee/student cards could be permitted entry into buildings with such devices.
